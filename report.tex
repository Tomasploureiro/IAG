% ══ ABSTRACT ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\begin{abstract}
This paper presents the implementation and comparative evaluation of three
generative image model families --- a Variational Autoencoder (VAE), a Deep
Convolutional Generative Adversarial Network (DCGAN), and a Denoising Diffusion
Probabilistic Model (DDPM) --- trained on the ArtBench-10 dataset
($32\times32$ pixels, 10 artistic styles). Models were first developed on a
20\% data subset and subsequently evaluated on the full 50,000-image training
set. Quantitative evaluation uses Fr\'echet Inception Distance (FID) and
Kernel Inception Distance (KID), each reported as mean~$\pm$~standard deviation
over 10 random seeds with 5,000 generated samples. The DCGAN achieved the best
results (FID~$=~30.46\pm0.57$; KID~$=~0.0032\pm0.0001$), followed by the DDPM
(FID~$=~37.91\pm0.37$; KID~$=~0.0062\pm0.0002$) and the VAE
(FID~$=~140.37\pm0.43$; KID~$=~0.0314\pm0.0004$). We analyse the trade-offs in
sample quality, diversity, training stability, and computational cost.

\keywords{Generative Models \and VAE \and DCGAN \and DDPM \and ArtBench-10 \and FID \and KID}
\end{abstract}

% ══ 1. INTRODUCTION ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Introduction}

Generative modelling of images has become a central research area, with
applications ranging from data augmentation to creative content synthesis.
Three paradigms have emerged as dominant: Variational Autoencoders
(VAE)~\cite{kingma2013}, Generative Adversarial Networks~\cite{radford2015},
and Diffusion Models~\cite{ho2020}, each offering distinct trade-offs between
sample fidelity, diversity, training stability, and inference cost.

Evaluating these models in the artistic domain is particularly challenging
because artworks exhibit complex textures, high style variability, and require
both visual realism and stylistic coherence. The ArtBench-10
dataset~\cite{liao2022} was designed precisely to benchmark generative models
in this setting, providing a curated, class-balanced collection of artwork
images across 10 historical styles.

This work addresses the task of learning generative models from artwork images
and producing new plausible samples that match the data distribution. We build
a complete generative pipeline --- covering data loading, preprocessing,
training, sampling, and evaluation --- and compare the three model families
under a controlled, reproducible protocol. All models were initially developed
on a 20\% data subset for rapid iteration and then trained on the full dataset
for final evaluation.

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows:
Section~\ref{sec:problem} describes the problem and dataset;
Section~\ref{sec:method} details the three model architectures;
Section~\ref{sec:setup} describes the experimental setup;
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents and analyses the results; and
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

% ══ 2. PROBLEM DESCRIPTION ════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Problem Description}
\label{sec:problem}

The goal of this project is image generation, not classification. Given a
dataset of artwork images, a generative model must learn the underlying data
distribution and produce new samples that are visually plausible and
statistically consistent with the training data.

\subsection*{Dataset: ArtBench-10}

ArtBench-10~\cite{liao2022} is a curated benchmark dataset for generative
modelling in the visual arts domain. It contains paintings grouped by artistic
style, specifically designed to evaluate models such as VAEs, GANs, and
diffusion models on structured artistic data. Its main characteristics are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Classes:} 10 artistic styles --- \textit{Art Nouveau},
        \textit{Baroque}, \textit{Expressionism}, \textit{Impressionism},
        \textit{Post-Impressionism}, \textit{Realism}, \textit{Renaissance},
        \textit{Romanticism}, \textit{Surrealism}, and \textit{Ukiyo-e}.
  \item \textbf{Size:} 50,000 training images and 10,000 test images
        (5,000/1,000 per class --- balanced).
  \item \textbf{Resolution:} $32\times32$ RGB.
\end{itemize}

This setting is challenging because models must simultaneously balance visual
fidelity, sample diversity, and style consistency. Unlike discriminative tasks,
quality must be assessed both qualitatively (sample inspection) and
quantitatively (distribution-level metrics).

\subsection*{Development Protocol}

Following the prescribed protocol, models were initially trained and tuned on a
provided \textbf{20\% data subset} (10,000 images) to allow fast iteration over
architectures and hyperparameters. All three model families were then retrained
on the \textbf{full training set} (50,000 images) for the final evaluation,
going beyond the minimum requirement of retraining only the best-performing
model.

% ══ 3. METHODOLOGY ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Variational Autoencoder (VAE)}

The VAE~\cite{kingma2013} learns a latent representation of the data by
maximising the Evidence Lower BOund (ELBO):
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta,\phi;\,x) =
    \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\!\left[\log p_\theta(x|z)\right]
    - D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z|x)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(0,I)\right),
\end{equation}
where the encoder $q_\phi(z|x)=\mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi,\sigma^2_\phi I)$ maps
inputs to a Gaussian posterior and the decoder $p_\theta(x|z)$ reconstructs
the input. Sampling is enabled by the reparameterisation trick
$z=\mu+\sigma\odot\varepsilon$, $\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$.

We use a $\beta$-VAE formulation with $\beta=0.5$ to weight reconstruction
more heavily than regularisation:
$\mathcal{L}_\beta = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{recon}} - \beta\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}$.

\textbf{Architecture.}
\textit{Encoder:} three strided conv.\ blocks
($3\to64\to128\to256$ channels, stride~2, BatchNorm, LeakyReLU~0.2),
then linear heads to $\mu,\log\sigma^2\in\mathbb{R}^{128}$.
\textit{Decoder:} three transposed-conv.\ blocks
($256\to128\to64\to3$, BatchNorm, ReLU, Sigmoid).

\subsection{Deep Convolutional GAN (DCGAN)}

GANs~\cite{radford2015} train a generator $G$ and discriminator $D$ via a
minimax objective:
\begin{equation}
  \min_G\max_D\;
  \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p_{\text{data}}}\!\left[\log D(x)\right]
  +\mathbb{E}_{z\sim p_z}\!\left[\log(1-D(G(z)))\right].
\end{equation}
In practice the generator maximises $\mathbb{E}_z[\log D(G(z))]$ to avoid
gradient saturation. DCGAN~\cite{radford2015} stabilises training via strided
convolutions (no pooling), BatchNorm, and LeakyReLU in the discriminator.

\textbf{Architecture.} The generator maps
$z\in\mathbb{R}^{100}$ through four transposed-convolutional blocks
($512{\times}4{\times}4 \to 256 \to 128 \to 3{\times}32{\times}32$, Tanh
output). The discriminator applies four strided convolutions
($3 \to 64 \to 128 \to 256 \to 1$, Sigmoid output, LeakyReLU~0.2).
Weights are initialised as $\mathcal{N}(0,0.02)$. Label smoothing sets
real labels to 0.9 to prevent discriminator overconfidence.

\subsection{Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM)}

DDPMs~\cite{ho2020} define a Markov forward process that adds Gaussian noise
over $T$ steps:
\begin{equation}
  q(x_t|x_0) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0,\;
  (1-\bar\alpha_t)I\right),\quad
  \bar\alpha_t=\textstyle\prod_{s=1}^{t}(1-\beta_s),
\end{equation}
and learn a neural network $\epsilon_\theta$ to reverse this process via:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_{t,x_0,\epsilon}\!\left[
    \bigl\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta\!\left(
      \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0+\sqrt{1{-}\bar\alpha_t}\,\epsilon,\,t
    \right)\bigr\|^2\right].
\end{equation}
Recent work~\cite{dhariwal2021} has shown that diffusion models can surpass
GANs in image quality at high resolutions. For efficient inference we apply
DDIM sampling~\cite{dhariwal2021} with 100 steps instead of the 1,000 training
steps, reducing generation time by a factor of~10.

\textbf{Architecture.} A U-Net with 29.60~M parameters operates at three
resolution levels ($32{\to}16{\to}8$ pixels). Each level contains two ResBlocks
(GroupNorm, SiLU, Conv2d $3{\times}3$, Dropout~0.1, residual connection).
4-head self-attention is applied at the $16{\times}16$ and $8{\times}8$ levels.
Timestep $t$ is encoded via a sinusoidal positional embedding followed by a
two-layer MLP and injected into every ResBlock via a linear projection.
An Exponential Moving Average (EMA, decay~$=0.9995$) of the weights is
maintained and used exclusively during sampling.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}

All images are resized and centre-cropped to $32\times32$ pixels. The VAE
normalises pixel values to $[0,1]$ (compatible with its Sigmoid output); the
DCGAN and DDPM normalise to $[-1,1]$ (Tanh and standard normalisation,
respectively). The DDPM additionally applies random cropping with a 4-pixel
margin, random horizontal flipping ($p=0.5$), and mild colour jitter
(brightness/contrast/saturation~$\pm5\%$, hue~$\pm2\%$) for data augmentation.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

The evaluation protocol follows the assignment specification exactly:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Samples:} 5,000 images generated by each model and 5,000 real
        images drawn from the test set.
  \item \textbf{FID:} computed on the full 5,000-sample sets using
        InceptionV3 features (2,048-dimensional, fc replaced by
        \texttt{Identity}).
  \item \textbf{KID:} computed as the mean MMD$^2$ over 50 random subsets of
        size~100 drawn from the generated and real feature sets; both the mean
        and standard deviation are reported per run.
  \item \textbf{Statistical robustness:} the full protocol is repeated for
        10 different random seeds (42--51). Final results are reported as
        mean~$\pm$~standard deviation of FID and mean~$\pm$~standard
        deviation of KID across the 10 runs.
  \item \textbf{Fairness:} preprocessing, sample count, and evaluation code
        path are identical for all models. DDIM sampling (100 steps) is used
        for the DDPM.
\end{itemize}

The VAE produces noticeably blurry images, a direct consequence of the
pixel-wise BCE reconstruction loss which averages over plausible outputs.
The DCGAN generates sharper, more detailed images but shows some
repetition of motifs, suggesting mild mode dropping. The DDPM produces the
most varied outputs, covering a wider range of styles and colour palettes,
reflecting the richer probabilistic formulation of the reverse diffusion
process.

The VAE training curves (Figure~\ref{fig:vaeloss}) confirm stable
convergence: the total loss decreased from~${\approx}1840$ to~${\approx}1780$
over 100 epochs, with the KL term stabilising at~${\approx}65$, indicating a
well-formed latent space.

\textbf{VAE.} The high FID ($140.37$) reflects the known limitation of
pixel-wise reconstruction losses: minimising the average reconstruction error
encourages blurry outputs, since the mean of multiple plausible sharp images
is itself blurry. The regularised latent space supports smooth interpolation
but does not enforce perceptual sharpness.

\textbf{DCGAN.} The adversarial objective directly minimises the perceptual
distance between real and generated distributions, explaining the strong FID
($30.46$) and KID ($0.0032$). At $32\times32$ resolution the discriminator's
capacity is sufficient to model the full feature space. The slightly larger FID
variance ($\pm0.57$) reflects the inherent stochasticity of the GAN training
dynamic. Mode dropping is visible qualitatively but not captured by FID/KID.

\textbf{DDPM.} The DDPM ranks between DCGAN and VAE (FID~$37.91$). Its lower
FID variance ($\pm0.37$) indicates more stable generation. The DDIM
approximation (100 vs.\ 1,000 steps) likely accounts for part of the gap with
DCGAN, as fewer denoising steps may not fully capture the learned distribution.
At higher resolutions diffusion models typically surpass GANs~\cite{dhariwal2021};
at $32\times32$ the DCGAN's simpler objective is sufficient to achieve better
distribution matching.

The very low standard deviations across all models and metrics confirm that
10 seeds with 5,000 samples provide statistically robust estimates, in line
with the evaluation protocol.

% ══ 6. CONCLUSION ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We implemented and compared three generative model families --- VAE, DCGAN,
and DDPM --- on the ArtBench-10 dataset at $32\times32$ resolution under a
rigorous, reproducible evaluation protocol. The DCGAN achieved the best
quantitative results on both FID and KID, benefiting from its adversarial
objective and the modest resolution of the task. The DDPM ranked second,
offering superior sample diversity at the cost of a slightly higher FID,
partly attributable to accelerated DDIM sampling. The VAE ranked last due to
the blurriness induced by pixel-wise reconstruction loss.

These results highlight a key trade-off: adversarial models excel at
distribution matching at low resolution, while diffusion models provide richer
diversity and are expected to scale better to higher resolutions~\cite{rombach2022}.
Future work includes evaluating DDPM with full 1,000-step sampling, training
class-conditional models, and extending to $64\times64$ or higher resolutions.
